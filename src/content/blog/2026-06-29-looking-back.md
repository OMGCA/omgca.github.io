All the motivational speeches are emphasising on not looking back — on looking forward instead. In general I do agree, but by doing that we often ignore how much we've achieved, while worrying about how far between us and the objective.

I'm that kind of nostalgic person who often enjoys chatting about 'the good old days', but I'm not stuck in the past either. So I progress, generally, definitely not top of the flock, but I do progress. And I do often worry about the uncertainty of the future, rather than be excited about it. When I'm positive sure that I have total control of certain things, that's when I get excited about the future, but you know, those occasions are extremely rare.

So I look back, from time to time, and I'm always amazed by how far I've gone. The progression is clear: school -> university -> workplace -> new workplace or promotion. When I look back at my university days, I often feel blessed by how much I've done differently from most of my classmates in school (of course, there are some folks that achieved way more than me). I think in most of time, I know what I want, and I have some sort of short and long term objectives. Those can be vague, but better than none.

But I also suffer from the dark times in the university, and that got me worried back in those days. I often think - maybe this is it, this is the top I can get. How would I outrun my surroundings in the future? I can't even imagine. But if I look back now, it was the exact same pattern - outrun most of them, some folks achieved way more. That'll do for me, and same pattern if we put the context of my previous workplace. But that was not even a year ago, so the pattern doesn't fully match for now.

None of those would be possible without my parents' support, and I'm referring to the university time. Studying abroad is a luxury that not many of us can enjoy, and I have absolutely no idea what I would end up without it. Maybe I can still manage to get admitted into some decent university by myself, but we never know.

But one thing's for sure: you gotta know what you want. It seems vague, but not really. I consider this my luckiest gift, that I was clear about what to do even in secondary school, that I want to become a software engineer. So now if I look back, none of the path would be correct - I wasn't doing too well in Math, and I majored in Electronics rather than Computer Science. But with that long-term objective in mind, at least I'm trying whatever I can to get into the top companies I admire (or admired), and I'm not stopping until I get to that level. Lucky enough, those were achieved right after my postgraduate studies, so the following objective was to survive in the workplace, which is something you need anyway.

And I reckon that sets me apart from most of my classmates. I clearly understand there are folks that way brighter, way more sociable than me, and I'm in no way competitive if we were in the same interview session. But it is what it is, what I felt like is most of them just simply fulfilling their current responsibilities, and same pattern emerged when I was in Huawei.

I guess 'knowing what you want' is the utmost important question a person can ask themselves.
